# GOWANA
## Brand Foundation Document
*Internal use. For founders, guides, and anyone who touches the brand.*

> This document exists so everyone building GOWANA builds the same thing, even when working alone.
>
> Read it once. Then keep it nearby.

## 1. What GOWANA Is

GOWANA is a tour and travel operation based in Sulawesi Tengah, Indonesia.

We take small groups of foreign travelers into places that don't appear on itineraries. The Togean Islands. Lore Lindu National Park. The megalithic stones of Bada Valley. The coastlines around Donggala. The karst islands of Sombori.

We do this as locals. We live here. This is home, not a product we resell from somewhere else. No corporate polish. No scripted speeches. Just people who love Sulawesi Tengah and want to show you why.

A portion of every tour goes directly into conservation work we're building alongside the business. Protecting what's already standing. No outside funding. No government dependency. Revenue from tours, reinvested into the place that makes the tours worth taking.

That's the model.

*Warning: may cause severe addiction to Sulawesi.*

## 2. The Name

**Gowana** comes from *wana* (ᨓᨊ), a word in the languages of Sulawesi Tengah: *forest*.

A forest is older than tourism. We hope it outlasts us by centuries.

*Go Wana* is a direction. Go deeper. Go into the forest. Go somewhere most people haven't been.

The name is a quiet commitment. We belong to this place. We don't extract from it.

## 3. The Amateur Thing

This gets its own section because people misread it more than anything else.

*Amateur* comes from the Latin *amare*: to love. Before the word got confused with incompetence, it described someone who showed up because they genuinely couldn't stay away.

That's what GOWANA means by it.

We'll have ten years of experience someday. We'll still call ourselves amateur. Because the moment you think you know everything, you stop discovering it.

### What It Looks Like

We don't pretend to have a perfect answer for everything. Our itineraries are plans, not contracts. We plan everything. Then nature, locals, and happy accidents make it better. That's not a flaw. That's the whole point.

Our guides share personal stories, not rehearsed scripts. When a traveler knows more about diving or birds or trekking than we do, that's a conversation, not a threat. We post the blurry photo if the moment was real. We tell you honestly when a road is bad, a boat is slow, or a plan fell apart.

### What It Does Not Mean

Amateur is not a disclaimer. It does not mean cutting corners on safety. It does not mean winging logistics that affect people's wellbeing. It does not mean being unprepared and calling it a vibe. It does not mean treating "we're figuring it out" as a substitute for competence.

The brand is amateur. The safety isn't. Certified guides. Proper equipment. Insurance. Emergency protocols. The safety briefing happens every time. We're funny, not reckless.

### The Internal Test

Before any decision, any post, any conversation with a traveler:

> *Is this honest, or is this performing?*

Performing expertise, performing humility, performing fun. None of that is GOWANA. Honesty probably is.

## 4. Who We're For

Foreign travelers, primarily from Australia, Europe, and North America. Millennials and Gen Z. Solo, in pairs, or in small groups.

They've been to Bali. They've done the obvious things. They want a place that hasn't been optimized for tourism yet, with a guide who actually knows it.

They pay attention to where their money goes. They're skeptical of greenwashing. When the impact is real and visible, it registers.

### What They're Afraid Of

This matters as much as knowing what they want.

Being ripped off by a middleman who's never set foot in the place. Being herded through a "local experience" designed for tourists. Paying for sustainability that doesn't exist. Being stuck with a guide who recites facts instead of knowing the land. Getting hit with hidden fees, surprise "local taxes," or awkward upselling at the end.

GOWANA's job is to make every one of those fears irrelevant. The difference shows from the first message. No promises required.

## 5. Voice and Tone

GOWANA sounds like a knowledgeable local friend. Someone who knows this place deeply, talks about it honestly, and doesn't need to impress you, because the place does that on its own.

### Four Registers

**Honest.**
We say what's true, including the uncomfortable parts. The road to Bada Valley is rough. The jungle is humid. The boat might run late. We say this upfront because we respect the traveler enough to prepare them properly.

**Curious.**
We're still learning this land. We say so. We ask questions back. When a traveler knows something we don't, we lean in. Curiosity keeps the experience alive.

**Local.**
We talk about Sulawesi Tengah the way someone talks about home. Specific. Personal. Occasionally biased. Travel brochure language has no place here. No "breathtaking." No "pristine." No "untouched paradise."

**Playful, but grounded.**
The humor exists. The self-awareness exists. The dry wit exists. None of it comes at the expense of clarity or trust. We're funny because we're honest, not because we're performing.

## 6. The Destinations

### Lore Lindu National Park
A UNESCO Biosphere Reserve. 2,180 km² of highland forest in the heart of Sulawesi Tengah. Seventy-seven bird species endemic to Sulawesi live here, including the Maleo, which buries its eggs in geothermally heated sand. The Anoa, a dwarf buffalo. The Babirusa, a deer-pig. Species that exist nowhere else. Megalithic stone statues of unknown origin are scattered across the highland valleys. Most travelers have never heard of this place.

### Bada Valley and Pokekea
Hundreds of megalithic statues and stone vessels called *kalamba*, undetermined age, scattered across remote highland valleys adjacent to Lore Lindu. Pokekea is accessible from Napu. Bada Valley demands more: rough roads, 4WD or trekking, or the longer route through Poso and Tentena. The effort is the point. Very little archaeological work has been done here. Nobody knows who built them.

### Togean Islands
Remote, biodiverse, and genuinely difficult to reach. That difficulty is exactly why the marine environment remains one of the most intact in the region. For the right traveler, unforgettable.

### Luwuk and the East Arm
Underrated and undervisited. Waterfalls, coastal scenery, and Danau Paisupok, a lake that went viral for good reason. A base for exploring the eastern arm of Sulawesi with a local who actually knows it.

### Sombori
Karst limestone islands rising from clear water in Morowali Regency. The comparison to Raja Ampat is fair above water. The marine life below is still being documented. Still early. Still raw. The landscape is extraordinary. Watch this space.

### Donggala and the West Coast
Coastal Sulawesi Tengah, close to Palu. Accessible, underexplored, genuinely beautiful. A good entry point for travelers who want to ease in before going deeper.

## 7. Visual Identity

### The Feeling Before the Specs

Raw. Real. Rooted.

The aesthetic should feel like field notes from someone who actually lives here. Grain overlays. Natural light. Real sweat. Cream backgrounds, not cold white. Type that has weight.

If a visual asset could belong to a generic travel brand, it doesn't belong to GOWANA.

The palette is warm, organic, slightly aged. Cold grays and pure whites don't belong here.

### Typography

**DM Serif Display.** Headlines, pull quotes, the logo wordmark. Warm, editorial, slightly old-world. Use italic for emotional emphasis, paired with Lime Light. Go large when it matters. This font earns scale.

**Inter.** All body copy, descriptions, UI text. Clean and readable. Weight 400 for paragraphs, 700+ for emphasis. The workhorse.

**Space Mono.** Labels, coordinates, tags, section numbers, metadata. Uppercase, wide letter-spacing. Field notes: quiet, precise. Use at 9–11px. Don't overuse.

### Photography

Real over polished. Always.

Natural light, not studio light. Unfiltered moments over composed shots. People who look like they're actually there, not posing for a brochure. The blurry photo of the whale shark beats the perfect stock image. If it looks like it came from a travel magazine, it's wrong for GOWANA.

## 8. What We Stand For

Three things. In this order.

**The place comes first.**
Every decision starts with one question: is this good for Sulawesi Tengah? What tours we run, who we partner with, how we price, what we say no to. Good for the place, not the business.

**The people who live here.**
Our guides live here. Our partners are local families. Every homestay, boat, restaurant, and guide we work with is locally owned and locally operated. Hard rule. Your money stays in the community. We don't even know where the tourist traps are.

**The travelers who come.**
We bring people into a place we love. They're participants, not passengers. That changes what we're willing to do and what we won't.

## 9. Pricing

What you see is what you pay.

No hidden fees. No surprise "local tax." No awkward upselling at the end. We hate that as much as you do.

The price includes what we say it includes. If something changes, we tell you before you pay, not after. Transparency isn't a selling point. It's the minimum.

## 10. The Conservation Work

The model is protect-first, not plant-first.

Sulawesi Tengah already has intact forest. Lore Lindu is a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve with 77 endemic bird species, 31 endemic rat species, and at least 55 species of bats. The Maleo, the Anoa, the Babirusa: they need the trees that are already standing to stay standing.

Our work starts there. Supporting the communities and corridors that keep existing forest intact. Reforestation is part of the toolkit. Protection is the headline.

### Non-Negotiables

Funded entirely from tour revenue. No government money. No outside grants at the early stage. No dependency on funding we don't control.

Documented from day one. Photos, GPS coordinates, real stories. Documentation is how it grows.

Travelers are invited to participate, never pressured. Conservation activities are optional add-ons, not mandatory components.

We never use the conservation work as a marketing prop without substance. If we say we protected a forest corridor, we show you the corridor.

As the business grows, the project grows. Eventually it becomes its own entity: its own website, its own donation channels, its own identity. For now, it lives inside GOWANA and is visible on every tour.

## 12. What We're Building

**Phase 1: Foundation.**
Brand identity locked. Website live. Instagram active. First tours run manually through WhatsApp inquiries, OTA listings, word of mouth.

**Phase 2: Proof.**
First two or three tours completed. Real photos. Real stories. Real testimonials. Conservation work begins: one partner, one location, documented from day one.

**Phase 3: Growth.**
Conservation project grows alongside tour revenue. The impact page fills with real data. Reputation builds on GetYourGuide, Viator, Airbnb Experiences. This phase takes as long as it takes.

**Phase 4: Separation.**
Conservation project becomes its own entity. Its own website, donation infrastructure, identity. GOWANA the tour operation and the conservation project become siblings.
